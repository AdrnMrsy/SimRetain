Predictive Dynamics of Employee Attrition

An Agent-Based Simulation App for Targeted Retention

Background of the Study

Employee turnover continues to be a major challenge for many organizations today. When employees leave frequently, companies experience financial losses and a decrease in overall productivity. Hiring new employees is not only costly but also time-consuming, as it involves recruitment, training, and adjustment periods.

Aside from the financial impact, high turnover can also affect team performance and workplace morale. When experienced employees leave, it disrupts workflows and creates gaps in knowledge and collaboration. Because of these issues, companies are now looking for better ways to understand and manage employee attrition more effectively.

Problem Statement

Human Resources (HR) departments often find it difficult to accurately predict when and why employees decide to leave. This is mainly because employee behavior is influenced by many factors such as job satisfaction, workload, salary, and personal circumstances.

Traditional prediction methods are usually too simple and cannot fully capture these complex and changing factors. As a result, companies struggle to create effective strategies to reduce employee turnover. There is a need for a more dynamic and realistic approach to better understand and predict employee attrition.

Objectives of the Project

Primary Goal:

The main goal of this project is to develop an Agent-Based Simulation model that can predict employee attrition in a more accurate and dynamic way.

Specific Objectives:

  • To identify the key factors that influence employees to leave their jobs.
  • To simulate different retention strategies and evaluate their effectiveness before applying them in real situations.

Scope & Limitations

🎯 Scope

This study focuses on employees in IT and Engineering departments, with a simulated population of around 500 employees.

The model will consider several important factors such as employee tenure, salary compared to market rates, commuting distance, overtime hours, and performance ratings.

🚧 Limitations

To keep the model manageable, it assumes that employees make rational decisions based on given factors.

The simulation does not include unexpected external factors such as economic crises, layoffs, or retirement. Because of this, the results may not fully represent all real-world situations.

Proposed System

SimRetain

An interactive tool designed for HR professionals to run different "what-if" scenarios and observe possible outcomes related to employee attrition.

By using this system, HR managers can test various strategies and make better decisions based on simulated results instead of relying only on assumptions or past data.

Conceptual Model

The simulation is based on three main components: Employees, Managers, and the Environment. Key factors include job satisfaction, burnout level, salary competitiveness, and length of service.

Logical Decision Rules (Examples):

Rule 1 (The Burnout Effect): IF an employee's burnout level reaches a critical high AND their salary is below the market average, THEN their probability of resigning increases significantly.

Rule 2 (The Loyalty Factor): IF an employee has a long length of service AND high job satisfaction, THEN their tolerance for temporary increases in workload is higher (low flight risk).

Rule 3 (The Environment Trigger): IF the overall work environment introduces negative changes (e.g., mandated excessive overtime), THEN job satisfaction decreases across the department.

Simulation Method

🎲

Stochastic Approach

The system runs on a monthly cycle, evaluating each employee's condition and assigning a probability of staying or leaving.

⚡

Random Elements

Changes in workload, satisfaction, or new job opportunities occur randomly, triggering the decision rules.

📊

Monte Carlo Method

The simulation runs multiple times. This averages out different outcomes, reducing errors and reliably representing real-life uncertainty.
